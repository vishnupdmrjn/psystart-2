Introduction: overview of the topic

This course delves into the multifaceted nature of identity development during the critical period of emerging adulthood. Building on foundational principles of human development, we will examine how various factors—such as social identity, cultural influences, emotional regulation, and selfconcept—intersect to shape the identities of young adults. Through an exploration of contemporary theories, such as Social Identity Theory and Ecological Systems Theory, students will gain insights into how group membership and environmental contexts inform identity exploration. The course emphasizes a holistic understanding of identity, highlighting how experiences during this stage can significantly impact individuals' selfconcept and relational dynamics.

Given the breadth of the topic, we will focus specifically on selected areas that illuminate the complexity of identity formation, including gender identity, sexual orientation, and the emotional processes that underpin selfconcept development. Throughout this semesterlong course, we aim to foster a nuanced understanding of how emerging adults navigate their identities amidst societal expectations and personal aspirations.

Course Objectives

This course will help learners:

Understand the theories of identity development as they apply to emerging adults.

Recognize the role of social and cultural contexts in shaping identity.

Appreciate the diversity of identity experiences among individuals in this age group.

Explore the implications of identity exploration for mental health and wellbeing.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of the course, students should be able to:

CO1: Articulate key concepts related to identity exploration in emerging adulthood.

CO2: Analyze the impact of social identity and cultural influences on personal identity development.

CO3: Examine the emotional regulation processes that contribute to selfconcept formation.

CO4: Apply developmental theories to understand realworld implications of identity issues.

CO5: Reflect critically on their own identity development and that of their peers.

Pedagogy:

Instruction will be dynamic and interactive, incorporating lectures, discussions, group activities, and reflective assignments. Each session will begin with a brief lecture to introduce key concepts, followed by group discussions that encourage students to engage actively with the material. Case studies, literature, and reallife scenarios will be used to enhance understanding and application of theories. Students are expected to take ownership of their learning by completing readings, participating in discussions, and reflecting on their experiences in relation to course content.

Formative assessments will be used to provide feedback on knowledge acquisition and engagement, helping to guide your learning journey throughout the course. Together, we will create a supportive environment where exploration and discussion are encouraged, allowing for a rich and rewarding learning experience.
